# Project 002 — Evaluating AI Reasoning on Startup Decision Making

## Objective

Evaluate how different AI models reason through an ambiguous startup scenario. The goal is to assess logical reasoning, trade-off analysis, and decision-making quality rather than factual accuracy.

---

## Prompt

> A startup has only $50,000 left in the bank.
>
> Should it prioritize hiring more engineers or acquiring more customers?
>
> Explain your reasoning.

---

## Responses

### Response A

There isn't a single correct answer. The right choice depends on what is currently preventing the company from surviving and growing.
A useful way to think about it is:
Invest in the biggest bottleneck.
Scenario 1: The product is already good enough, but customers are lacking
The startup should generally prioritize customer acquisition.
Why:
Revenue extends the company's runway.
Customer feedback validates product-market fit.
More users make the company more attractive to investors.
Additional engineers won't help if no one is using the product.
For example, if the startup has a working SaaS product with happy early users but only a few dozen customers, spending the remaining cash on sales, marketing, or partnerships is often the higher-return investment.
Scenario 2: The product is not yet usable
The startup should prioritize engineering.
Why:
If the product is buggy, unreliable, or missing essential features, acquiring customers may be wasteful because they'll churn quickly.
A strong product creates a better foundation for future growth.
However, even then, hiring should be targeted. A $50,000 cash balance is a small runway, so hiring multiple full-time engineers may not be feasible.
The financial reality
With only $50,000 remaining:
The startup likely has only a few months of runway, depending on burn rate.
Every hiring decision is expensive and increases fixed costs.
Hiring is difficult to reverse if revenue doesn't materialize.
Because of this, preserving runway is usually critical.
A balanced approach
Many founders would avoid making a permanent hire immediately and instead:
Keep the engineering team lean.
Fix only the highest-priority product issues.
Focus the majority of effort on getting paying customers.
Use contractors or freelancers for specific engineering tasks if necessary.
My conclusion
If the product is already good enough to deliver value, I would prioritize acquiring more customers. A startup without customers has no business, while customer traction can generate revenue, validate demand, and make future fundraising or hiring much easier.
If the product is not yet capable of retaining customers, then engineering should come first—but only enough to remove the key product bottleneck before shifting focus back to growth.
In short, the decision should be driven by the startup's primary constraint, not by a general rule.

### Response B

With only $50,000 left in runway, this startup is in critical, survival-mode territory. Assuming a standard burn rate, $50,000 provides mere weeks—or at best, 2–3 months—of existence.
At this exact moment, the startup must prioritize acquiring customers (and generating immediate revenue) over hiring engineers.

Why Acquiring Customers Wins
1. Extending Survival (Runway)
Customers bring cash in; engineers send cash out. Hiring even a single full-time engineer can consume $10,000–$15,000 per month (salary, taxes, equipment). That single hire would drain the remaining $50k in under 4 months—with zero guarantee of generating revenue before the bank account hits $0.
Immediate Revenue: Every new paying customer directly extends the startup's runway, turning a counting-down clock into a sustainable business.
2. Fundability Requires Validation
If the company needs to raise seed or venture funding to survive, investors almost never invest in "we built a cool product with 10 engineers." They invest in traction, customer demand, and product-market fit.
Demonstrating that real people are paying for the existing product proves value. A buggy or basic product with paying customers is fundable; a polished product with zero customers is a dead startup.
3. Focus on What Matters
Building more features without sufficient customer feedback risks building the wrong things. Acquiring customers forces the current team to learn what users actually care about and pay for.
When Would Hiring Engineers Make Sense? (Exceptions)
The only scenarios where engineering investment takes priority at this stage are rare and conditional:
The Core Product Is Unusable: If the product literally breaks during onboarding or fails to deliver its core promise, spending on marketing/sales is burning cash on a leaky bucket. However, instead of permanent hires, the founders should fix critical bugs themselves or bring in temporary contractors.
Pre-sold Contracts Require Delivery: If the startup has signed Enterprise MOUs or LOIs with massive upfront cash deposits contingent on specific technical deliverables, short-term engineering execution pays for itself directly.


Immediate Action Plan for the $50k
Instead of traditional hiring or expensive ad campaigns:
Founders Do Sales: Founders should handle sales and acquisition directly via direct outreach, cold emails, warm network intro calls, and organic content (0 additional salary cost).
Cap Marketing Spend: Spend tiny, highly targeted amounts on direct-response channels where customer acquisition cost (CAC) is easily tracked and lower than customer lifetime value (LTV).
Outsource/Contract Minimal Repairs: If product fixes are desperately needed, use short-term freelance contracts to unblock revenue rather than adding permanent payroll overhead.
Bottom Line: Cash flow is the oxygen of a startup. When you are down to $50,000, your sole focus must be acquiring oxygen (revenue) before you try to build a bigger engine (hiring).

### Response C

With only $50,000 left, this is really a question of runway and survival, so the honest answer is: it depends on what's actually killing you right now — but in most cases at this stage, acquiring customers (or at least validating that customers will pay) should come first. Here's the reasoning:
Why customers usually win at this stage
$50k doesn't buy much engineering time. One or two senior engineers will burn through that in a couple of months once you factor in salary, taxes, benefits, and equipment. If you hire without more revenue or funding lined up, you could be flat broke before the new hires even ship anything meaningful.


Customers (or paying pilots) generate cash and proof. Revenue extends your runway directly. Even a handful of new paying customers can be the difference between shutting down and reaching your next milestone. It also de-risks the business in the eyes of future investors — "we have paying customers" is a far stronger fundraising story than "we hired more engineers."


Most startups don't die from lack of code — they die from lack of demand. If the product already does what customers need, the bottleneck usually isn't engineering headcount; it's sales, marketing, onboarding, or customer success. Pouring the last of your cash into more building when the real gap is distribution is a classic failure mode.


When hiring engineers would actually be the right call
If you already have paying customers lined up or a strong pipeline, but the product is missing a critical feature or is too buggy/unscalable to close deals or retain users — then the bottleneck genuinely is engineering, and not hiring could cost you those customers.
If you're pre-product and have no functioning product to sell at all, you may need at least a minimal viable product before customer acquisition spending makes sense.
A practical way to decide
Ask: "If I had one more of X (an engineer or a customer), would it move me closer to revenue or funding in the next 60–90 days?" With $50k, you likely have 1–3 months of runway. Whatever choice doesn't produce a measurable result in that window is the wrong one.
A middle path many cash-strapped startups take: skip new hires entirely, have the existing team spend a chunk of their time on outbound sales/customer development rather than pure engineering, and use the $50k almost entirely toward things that generate revenue or bridge to your next funding round (extending runway, running lean, maybe a part-time or contract salesperson instead of a full engineer).
If you can share a bit more — do you have a working product already, and do you have any revenue or committed customers today? — I can give you a sharper recommendation.

## Key Takeaways

This exercise reinforced that evaluating AI-generated reasoning is not about agreeing with a conclusion but about assessing the quality of the reasoning process. The strongest responses identified uncertainty, explored multiple scenarios, considered trade-offs, and avoided unsupported assumptions before making recommendations.

---

## Reflection

### What I Learned

- Strong AI responses acknowledge uncertainty when important context is missing.
- Practical recommendations should be supported by clear reasoning rather than confident assertions.
- Evaluating trade-offs is often more valuable than identifying a single "correct" answer.

### What I'd Do Differently

- Support more evaluation points with direct evidence from each response.
- Be more conservative when assigning perfect scores.
- Focus more on identifying subtle differences between high-quality responses.
